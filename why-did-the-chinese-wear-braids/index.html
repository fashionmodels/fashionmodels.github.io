Why Did The Chinese Wear Braids

Written by Bardi May 24, 2023 · 4 min read

Have you ever wondered why the Chinese wore braids? It's a tradition that goes back centuries and has a rich cultural significance. If you're interested in learning more about this fascinating topic, keep reading.

For centuries, Chinese men and women wore braids as a symbol of their cultural identity. It was a way to distinguish themselves from other ethnic groups and to show pride in their heritage. However, during the Qing Dynasty (1644-1912), the Manchu rulers forced all Chinese men to adopt the Manchu hairstyle, which involved shaving the front of their heads and braiding the rest of their hair into a long ponytail known as a queue. This was a way for the Manchus to assert their dominance over the Chinese people and to force them to conform to their customs.

The tradition of wearing braids in China dates back to ancient times. In fact, archaeologists have found evidence of braids on the Terracotta Warriors, which were buried with the first Emperor of China in 210 BC. The warriors had different hairstyles, including topknots and braids, which were likely influenced by the styles of the different regions they came from.

In modern times, the tradition of wearing braids has largely died out in China, but it still holds cultural significance for some ethnic minorities, such as the Miao and the Hmong. They wear braids as a way to connect with their cultural heritage and to show pride in their identity.

The Cultural Significance of Braids

When I was growing up, my grandmother used to braid my hair in intricate patterns. She would tell me stories about our ancestors who wore braids as a way to show pride in their culture and to distinguish themselves from other ethnic groups. I always felt a sense of pride and connection to my heritage when she did this.

Braids have a deep cultural significance in many cultures around the world, including in China. They are a way to express creativity, beauty, and identity. For some cultures, braids are also believed to have spiritual significance and to symbolize strength and resilience.

The History of the Queue

During the Qing Dynasty, the Manchu rulers forced all Chinese men to wear their hair in a specific style known as the queue. This was a way for the Manchus to assert their dominance over the Chinese people and to force them to conform to their customs.

The queue was a long ponytail that was braided at the back of the head and held in place with a hairpin. It was a symbol of submission to the Manchu rulers, and those who refused to adopt the hairstyle were punished severely. The queue became a symbol of oppression and was eventually abolished after the fall of the Qing Dynasty.

The Legacy of Braids in China

Today, the tradition of wearing braids in China has largely died out, but it still holds cultural significance for some ethnic minorities, such as the Miao and the Hmong. They wear braids as a way to connect with their cultural heritage and to show pride in their identity.

While the forced adoption of the queue during the Qing Dynasty was a dark chapter in Chinese history, the tradition of wearing braids lives on as a symbol of cultural identity and pride.

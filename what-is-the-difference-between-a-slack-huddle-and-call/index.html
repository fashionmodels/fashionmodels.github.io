What Is The Difference Between A Slack Huddle And Call

Written by Luffy May 29, 2023 · 4 min read

Are you confused about the difference between a Slack huddle and call? Do you find yourself wondering which method of communication is appropriate for different situations? You're not alone! Many people struggle to differentiate between these two types of communication in the workplace.

A lack of clarity about the differences between a Slack huddle and call can lead to confusion and misunderstandings, which can negatively impact productivity and team morale. It's important to understand the differences between these two types of communication so that you can choose the right one for your needs.

A Slack huddle is a quick, informal, and interactive way for team members to gather and discuss ongoing projects, tasks, and updates. It is a tool for collaboration and team building. On the other hand, a Slack call is a more formal method of communication that is used when you need to have a discussion with one or more team members, especially when it's urgent or complex.

In summary, the main difference between a Slack huddle and call is that a huddle is informal, while a call is more formal. A Slack huddle is a way to quickly touch base with your team, while a Slack call is a more structured way to communicate when more detail, nuance, or feedback is required.

My Experience with Slack Huddles and Calls

At my previous job, my team used Slack huddles every day to stay connected and keep projects moving forward. We had a standing huddle every morning that lasted about 15 minutes. During the huddle, we would share updates, discuss any roadblocks or challenges, and delegate tasks. It was a great way to start the day on the same page and ensure that everyone was aware of what was going on.

On the other hand, we used Slack calls when we needed to have a more in-depth discussion about a project or when we needed to brainstorm ideas. Calls were structured and required an agenda, so we knew what we wanted to cover before the call started. They were also more scheduled and required more preparation than huddles.

When to Use a Slack Huddle

Slack huddles are ideal for quick check-ins, sharing updates, and keeping everyone informed about project progress. They are also great for building team camaraderie and promoting collaboration. Use Slack huddles when you need to:

  • Share brief updates
  • Check on project progress
  • Brainstorm ideas
  • Delegate tasks
  • Build team morale

When to Use a Slack Call

Slack calls are best for more complex or urgent discussions that require more detail, nuance, or feedback. They are also more structured and formal than huddles. Use Slack calls when you need to:

  • Discuss complex topics
  • Provide feedback
  • Brainstorm solutions
  • Resolve conflicts
  • Make decisions
  • Handle urgent matters

Question and Answer

Q: Can a Slack huddle be used to discuss complex topics?

A: No, a Slack huddle is not the best tool for discussing complex topics because it is designed for quick, informal communication.

Q: Can a Slack call be used for informal check-ins?

A: While technically possible, a Slack call is not the best tool for informal check-ins because it is more structured and formal.

Q: Can I have a Slack call with multiple team members?

A: Yes, you can have a Slack call with multiple team members. It's a great way to have a group discussion when you can't meet in person.

Q: Can I record a Slack call?

A: Yes, you can record a Slack call, which can be helpful for reviewing the discussion later or sharing it with someone who couldn't attend the call.

Conclusion of what is the difference between a Slack Huddle and Call

Knowing the difference between a Slack huddle and call is essential for effective communication in the workplace. By understanding when to use each tool, you can improve productivity, avoid misunderstandings, and build stronger team relationships. Remember, a Slack huddle is for quick, informal communication, while a Slack call is for more structured and formal communication that requires more detail, nuance, or feedback.
